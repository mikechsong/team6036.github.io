Join Us

As a community organization, Team 6036 prides itself in being extremely open: we take anyone who is interested in contributing to the technical education of themselves or others in the community, and no time commitment is required. This also means that no previous knowledge of anything robotics or management related is necessary to join -- you just have to be in high school. That said, few people can jump into robotics and learn every aspect of the field without serious time and effort. This is why we have subteams: specialized groups of members that work on a specific part of the necessary components of the robot. As a team, we are vaguely divided into Software, Build, Design, and Business. Note that you're not forced to be in a single one or any of these subteams to contribute, but they are organized and led separately, as their tasks are vastly different.


Software

The part of the team most detached from the physical robot, the Software subteam's responsibilities include maintaining the website, active codebases (sets of code that the team may reuse), and demo robot's functionality (for sponsors and friendly matches), on top of making sure that the robot designed, funded, and assembled by the rest of the team can actually be used.

While learning to coding and computer science are a field of their own, they are essential to robotics. New members with no knowledge are welcome to join this subteam, and will be led through the basics on how the basic tools we use (git, java, wpilib, VS Code) work.

Build

The build subteam is composed of almost everyone on the team. Very few people don't play a role in assembling some portion of the robot, whether it be a gearbox or a flywheel. Of course, this is also the subteam with the greatest workload. Once the designs are finalized, this subteam is in charge of machining the necessary parts, assembling them, being present to help software test their functionality and stability, working around any design flaws, and replacing any broken parts as they wear out.

Note that the machining stage for parts will only be done by members that have been thoroughly trained in the use of whatever given machine is needed. Any member that wants this training will receive it, but they will not be permitted to use the machines (especially the lathe/mills) until cleared.

Design

The Design subteam is in charge of, as you would assume, designing the robot. They are arguably the most crucial subteam, as if the designs take too long, are too difficult to machine or assemble, are faulty, or do not meet tolerances, the robot simply will not have the capability to perform in a competition. The majority of design's work is done in cad software (solidworks or fusion 360), but their role also includes coming up with and proposing to the team what sort of strategy and mechanisms the year's robot will use.

Any new members wanting to learn how to cad will be taught to do so and given a license for the software (or shown how to get one), given that their computer meets the software's system requirements (not actually very high for fusion, but something to consider).

Business

The Business team is the smallest, yet most important subteam. If you look at the sponsors list on this site, you will see the product of this subteam. They get money for the team. The yearly cost of entry to the FRC competition is about $6000, which for high schoolers, is not easy to get. On top of this, the cost of the parts in a robot can cost from $3000 to $5000, not counting the machinery, replacement parts, travel expenses, etc. Without this subteam, 6036 would not be a sustainable team, and the community would lose an important source of education.
